## Company Overview

### Founding & Evolution

Veeva Systems was founded in 2007 by Peter Gassner, Matt Wallach, Doug Ostler, and Mitch Wallace, emerging from a critical insight: pharmaceutical and biotech companies needed specialized cloud-based software for regulatory publishing, clinical trial management, and customer relationship management that generic platforms could not provide. Gassner, drawing on 30+ years of enterprise software experience (IBM, PeopleSoft, Salesforce), recognized the opportunity to build purpose-built vertical SaaS for life sciences.

The company launched its flagship product, Veeva CRM, in 2008 on the Salesforce cloud platform, enabling pharmaceutical sales representatives to access healthcare provider data within a compliant interface. The critical inflection point arrived in February 2011 with the introduction of Veeva Vault, described as the first cloud-based regulated content management system specifically designed for the life sciences industry. By September 2012, Vault expanded into the enterprise market with a comprehensive suite of content management applications. Veeva went public on October 16, 2013, at $20 per share, raising $217 million and experiencing an 83.5% first-day pop to $36.70, valuing the company at $5.25 billion.

The company expanded aggressively through acquisitions: Zinc Ahead (2015, sales intelligence), Physicians World (November 2019, healthcare provider data), Learnaboutgmp (August 2021, GxP eLearning), and Veracity Logic (December 2021, randomization and trial supply management). A strategic turning point came in November 2022 when Veeva announced the end of its Salesforce partnership, effective September 2025, marking a fundamental shift in architecture. This pivot accelerated with the January 2024 general availability of Vault CRM—a next-generation CRM platform built on proprietary Vault architecture featuring innovative applications including CRM Bot and Service Center. As of Q3 FY2025, 30+ companies have adopted Vault CRM, with major pharmaceutical companies planning to migrate thousands of users by end of 2025. In November 2024, Veeva announced GenAI features including Vault CRM Bot and Voice Control capabilities, positioning the platform for the AI-driven era.

### Business Model

Veeva operates a focused vertical SaaS business model with two revenue streams: Subscription Services (83% of FY2024 revenue) and Professional Services (17%). In fiscal 2024, total revenue reached $2,363.7 million (+10% YoY), with subscription revenues of $1,901.6 million (+10% YoY). The trajectory is accelerating: Q3 FY2025 revenue totaled $699.2 million (+13% YoY), with subscription services accelerating to $580.9 million (+17% YoY), driven by AI adoption and Vault CRM migration momentum.

Subscription revenue is generated through recurring monthly or annual fees based on user count, feature scope, and application access. This recurring revenue model supports predictable cash generation and customer lifetime value optimization. Professional services include implementation, integration, customization, training, and ongoing support—generating high-margin revenue while deepening customer switching costs.

Veeva's customer base comprises 1,037 life sciences companies globally, including 47 of the top 50 pharmaceutical companies and 85% of the top 100 pharma companies. While the top 20 customers represent 38% of revenue—indicating concentration risk—the customer base is diversified across large enterprises, mid-market, and emerging biotech. Approximately 87% of revenue derives from the life sciences and pharmaceutical industries, reflecting Veeva's disciplined vertical focus.

### Competitive Position

Veeva commands the largest share of the specialized life sciences CRM and content management market, serving 19 of the world's top 20 life sciences companies. Key competitors include IQVIA (16.3% market share in broader life sciences software, primarily focused on CRO services using Salesforce infrastructure), Microsoft, Salesforce, Dassault Systèmes (owner of Medidata, focused on clinical trials), and Oracle. Veeva's competitive differentiation lies in four dimensions:

**Vertical Specialization:** Few competitors offer integrated suites spanning CRM, clinical trials, regulatory, quality, and content management with deep industry regulatory knowledge. IQVIA focuses on CRO services; Medidata serves clinical trials; Salesforce and Oracle are broad-based generalists lacking pharma-specific compliance expertise.

**Switching Costs:** Once pharmaceutical companies embed Veeva across sales, marketing, clinical, and regulatory functions, the integrated nature of the platform creates prohibitively high migration costs—training, data migration, workflow reconfiguration, and regulatory recertification.

**Network Effects:** An ecosystem of Veeva-trained employees and certified consultants reinforces the company's status as the industry standard, creating self-reinforcing adoption.

**Proprietary Integration:** The 2024 migration from Salesforce to proprietary Vault CRM strengthens the moat by eliminating platform dependency and creating a cohesive ecosystem that generic platforms cannot replicate.

The global life sciences software market is projected to reach $40.4 billion by 2033 (CAGR 10.5%), with cloud-based deployments capturing an increasing share (64.7% of market in 2023, expected to grow faster than on-premises). Veeva's vertical positioning and migration to proprietary Vault CRM position the company to capture above-market growth rates.

Competitive pressure from Salesforce's Life Sciences Cloud launch in 2025 represents a risk, though Veeva's deep vertical expertise and integrated platform create defensibility. The primary threat is not head-to-head competition but the possibility of large customers developing in-house solutions or adopting competing platforms—a risk worth monitoring.
